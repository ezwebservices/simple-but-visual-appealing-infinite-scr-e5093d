## Post 1: Launch Announcement

**What happens when you design TikTok for a 4-year-old learning math?**

You get NumPals.

We just launched an infinite-scroll math app for preschoolers (ages 3-5) where every swipe is a 10-15 second micro-lesson starring animal characters.

No reading required. No menus. No worksheets disguised as games.

Just swipe, tap, count, and play with animal pals while math quietly happens in the background.

Here's what designing for a pre-reading 4-year-old forced us to rethink:

**Sound is the primary interface.** Every number is spoken. Every tap has audio feedback. The background is soft xylophone in major key. At this age, ears lead and eyes follow.

**Touch targets are 64px minimum.** We tested with real kids. Anything smaller caused frustration and abandonment within seconds.

**There is no "wrong."** Wrong taps get "Almost! Try again!" with a gentle sound. We never show a red X to a 4-year-old. Joy is the only metric.

**Characters drive engagement, not gamification.** Kids don't care about points. They care about Sunny the Lion, Rosie the Bunny, and Milo the Monkey. Character attachment is the retention engine.

**The scroll IS the product.** No home screen. No settings menu. No level select. You open the app and start swiping. Friction is the enemy of a toddler's attention span.

The result: kids who think they're playing with animal friends, parents who get guilt-free screen time, and math skills (counting 1-20, addition and subtraction to 10) that develop through pure play.

If you're building products for young children or interested in early childhood education design, I'd love to connect and exchange ideas.

## Post 2: Design Lessons

**5 things I learned designing an app for users who can't read:**

We built NumPals, a math app for 3-5 year olds. Our user can't read, has a 15-second attention span, and interacts with the world through sound, color, and touch.

Here's what that taught us:

**1. Audio is accessibility, not a feature.**
For pre-readers, if it doesn't make a sound, it doesn't exist. We narrate every number, every instruction, every celebration. Our app would work with your eyes closed.

**2. Negative feedback breaks everything.**
Adults can handle a red X. A 4-year-old interprets it as "I'm bad at this" and stops playing. We replaced all failure states with gentle encouragement. Engagement went up dramatically.

**3. Menus are a dead end.**
Our first prototype had a character select screen. Kids couldn't navigate it. We deleted the entire concept. Now the app opens and you swipe. That's it.

**4. Characters > points > badges > streaks.**
Every gamification playbook is written for adults. Kids form emotional attachments to characters. That attachment is more powerful than any point system.

**5. The best UX is the one they already know.**
Kids already swipe on phones. We didn't teach a new interaction — we borrowed one they'd already mastered and filled it with math.

Building for the youngest users strips away every assumption about "good" product design. What's left is surprisingly universal.

## Post 3: Parent-Focused

**The screen time conversation is broken.**

It's always framed as "screen time = bad" vs. "screen time = unavoidable."

What if there's a third option: screen time that's genuinely, measurably educational — and the child doesn't even realize they're learning?

We built NumPals for our own kids because we couldn't find this. A math app for 3-5 year olds that:

- Works without reading (everything is visual + audio)
- Has absolutely no ads, links, or social features
- Teaches real math skills (counting, addition, subtraction)
- Uses characters and sound to create engagement without manipulation
- Lets the child navigate independently

The result surprised us. Our daughter doesn't ask for "the math app." She asks to "play with Sunny and Rosie." And then she counts to 20, practices 3+2, and celebrates with confetti.

The screen time debate needs more options like this. Not less screen time or more screen time — better screen time.
